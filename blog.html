Meshmixer
Why the Mirror Technique Is the Foundation of This Workflow
Most of the design process on this site comes down to one core idea: the human skull is roughly symmetric, and that symmetry can be used to reconstruct a missing region. In Meshmixer, this means enabling Symmetry, selecting the defect boundary with a brush, and letting the software generate a matching patch from the healthy side.
It sounds simple, but getting a clean result takes practice — the mirrored patch rarely aligns perfectly on the first try, and real skulls are never perfectly symmetric. Most of the actual design time in this project went into aligning, sculpting, and refining that mirrored patch until it sat naturally against the surrounding bone.
Sachin B. Khaire

About This Project
Why I Built Cranial3DLab
This site started from what I learned during my internship in the Additive Manufacturing department at Orthotech India Pvt. Ltd., combined with coursework in Biomaterials & Implants during my M.Tech at IIT Guwahati. I found that a lot of the practical, step-by-step knowledge around cranial plate design in Meshmixer wasn't easy to find in one place — it was scattered across forums, papers, and trial-and-error.
Cranial3DLab is my attempt to put that workflow together clearly, using only free and open-source tools like 3D Slicer and Meshmixer. It's built purely for educational and research purposes, aimed at students and enthusiasts who want to understand how patient-specific implant design actually works, hands-on.
Sachin B. Khaire

Design Notes
Solid vs. Lattice: Choosing a Plate Structure
Across the case studies on this site, you'll notice two different approaches: some plates use a Voronoi lattice pattern, while others use a solid continuous surface. This wasn't arbitrary — smaller defects were a good fit for lattice patterns, which reduce material and weight while keeping the design visually interesting to study.
For a larger defect, a solid surface made more sense in this project, simply to keep the modeling and printing process more straightforward for a large coverage area. Both approaches are shown here mainly to demonstrate the range of what Meshmixer's pattern and surface tools can do — not as a clinical recommendation for which is "better."
Sachin B. Khaire
